Global AI Policy & Governance: Competition and Control

Geopolitical competition around AI capability and governance intensified this week, characterized by divergent strategic pathways: industrial control in Europe versus market acceleration in the United States. The European Union announced the **"Apply AI Strategy"** and expansion of the **"AI Factories"** network with €204 million in funding. This initiative represents a decisive shift toward promoting European-made AI tools across key national strategic areas to reduce dependency on powerful US and Chinese platforms. The EU is strategically pairing its stringent regulatory environment (the AI Act) with state-sponsored industrial policy, aiming to use its high compliance bar as a competitive advantage. In contrast, the US federal government, guided by the "America's AI Action Plan," prioritizes rapid technological leadership through **deregulation and accelerated federal permitting** for compute infrastructure, ensuring the fastest possible buildout. This federal focus on speed creates regulatory gaps, which are being filled at the state level: all 50 US states and territories have introduced AI-related legislation in the 2025 session, with **38 states enacting approximately 100 measures**. These state-level laws, such as Arkansas's on IP ownership and Montana's "Right to Compute" law, create a complex **50-state compliance calculus** for national companies, making nationwide deployment riskier than operating solely within the EU's single, codified framework.

Societal & Economic Impact: The Skill Acceleration Cliff

The maturation of AI is reinforcing a deeply polarized economic landscape, creating massive value for the skilled few while accelerating displacement for routine tasks. The labor market is experiencing intense distortion, with AI adoption accounting for **over 10,000 US job cuts** in the first seven months of 2025, solidifying AI as a primary displacement factor. In sharp contrast, workers with specialized AI skills, such as prompt engineering and AI auditing, command a monumental **56% wage premium** over peers, a rate that more than doubled over the previous year. This extreme premium is not merely market demand, but a reflection of a **severe structural bottleneck**—the inability of upskilling systems to keep pace with the accelerating demand for expertise. The pace of necessary skill change in AI-exposed jobs is accelerating **66% faster than last year**, forcing business leaders to acknowledge they are entering "one of the largest change management exercises in history". Furthermore, the focus of AI safety has shifted from theoretical existential risk to concrete, immediate harm reduction. This is evident in the **FSB’s warning** that compute supply chain concentration poses a **systemic financial risk** to global financial stability. Corporate action, such as **OpenAI’s report detailing the disruption of over 40 malicious networks** used by authoritarian regimes for coercion and influence, confirms that threat actors are generally "bolting AI onto old playbooks to move faster".
